  The search for the historical Mulan



What is the true story of Hua Mulan?

This is a question many have asked, but the answers are never satisfactory. Most people have been left with the impression that there is no evidence for her life. On the contrary, numerous clues have been left behind, all of which point to the fact that Mulan’s story has a thread of truth in it. While historians doubt Mulan’s existence since her history cannot be conclusively proved, a diligent search for the historical Mulan reveals the touching true story of a girl who sacrificed everything for the sake of her ailing father and went on to become a legendary inspiration to countless generations around the globe.

Although there are some uncertainties in the historical accounts, we can be reasonably convinced that this account of her life is true.

Mulan’s story is typically set during a time when the era of the Sixteen Kingdoms was drawing to a close and Northern Wei was rising to power. Thus, if we are to search for evidence of her life, we should begin our investigation by consulting the Book of Wei, which is one of the most authoritative ancient texts on the history of Northern Wei.



Although the Book of Wei does not mention Mulan by name, it does contain some essential hints which will enable us to piece her story together.

The Ballad of Mulan states that Mulan went to war and returned triumphantly twelve years later. Because it was not customary for Chinese rulers to conscript old and sick men for just any battle, we would expect the beginning of Mulan’s story to take place during a time when China was engaged in (or preparing for) an especially massive war. Thus, if the story is true, we should expect to find historical records that a major campaign was launched, followed by a landslide victory twelve years later.

The most likely candidate for the year in which Mulan joined the army is 429 AD when Emperor Taiwu of Northern Wei launched a major attack against Rouran. This led to a series of campaigns, which concluded twelve years later when Emperor Taiwu united all of Northern China in 441 AD and brought the era of the Sixteen Kingdoms to a close.

Shortly before Emperor Taiwu attacked Rouran, a commander by the name of Mu Chong fell ill and retired from the army. It seems strange that he would end his military career at a time when such large-scale invasion was about to take place. Emperor Taiwu was a bloodthirsty warlord who won battles through attrition. It was not uncommon for him to suffer significant losses—up to half of his soldiers in a single battle—and then conscript additional men to make up for the difference. Emperor Taiwu’s men did not retire. They were killed in action. During a time when one of the dynasty’s most significant accomplishments lay on the horizon, it is highly unlikely that Mu Chong would have been allowed to retire unless someone had volunteered to take his place.

While the Book of Wei does not mention any of Mu Chong’s children who could have replaced him, the Northern Dynasties Surname Investigation (北朝姓氏考) reveals that there was a daughter of the Mu family whose name was Mu Lan.

Could Mu Lan have been the filial daughter who became the legendary hero we now know as Mulan? Even though Mu Lan is a full name and Mulan is a given name, their pronunciations are identical. In a society where only the elite could read and write, the two would easily be confused. More importantly, Mulan (木蘭) is a Han Chinese name while Mu Lan (穆蘭) is a Xianbei name.



The Xianbei were once a nomadic people who, before conquering China, dwelt in what is now present-day Mongolia. The fall of the Jin Dynasty ushered in the era of the Sixteen Kingdoms, which was one of the darkest and most turbulent times in China’s history. Eventually, the Xianbei rose and subdued the Chinese.

Even though the Xianbei were in power during this time, the Han Chinese people far outnumbered the Xianbei. As the legend of Mulan was passed down from generation to generation, the Chinese people, who were in the majority, would have naturally chosen to depict Mulan as one of their own. Thus, when the legend was finally written down, the Chinese name was chosen.

Even if Mulan’s ethnicity was Xianbei and not Han Chinese, this doesn’t change the fact that she was an incredibly filial daughter who left an inspiring example for us to follow. Even though there existed much tension between the Han Chinese and Xianbei people, Emperor Taiwu, in his thirst for power, oppressed everyone equally. Because he had no problems with sending Xianbei men to their deaths, the story of a woman needing to take her father’s place to save his life works equally well no matter what the heroine’s ethnicity was. Even if Mulan was Xianbei, she remains a hero to be honored by every culture.

While this attempt to reconstruct Mulan’s life may seem highly speculative at first, we will soon see that there is more evidence to support this version of Mulan’s story than other retellings which have gained popularity in recent years.

If we reread the Ballad of Mulan through Mu Lan’s eyes, we will see a whole new story emerge. The author and date of the Ballad are unknown, although it is typically assumed to have been written during the Tang dynasty (which would mean that it was penned over a century after Mu Lan’s death). However, it is very possible that it could have been composed much earlier.

Cao Xi from Qiqihar University argues that the Ballad was authored by Mu Lan herself (木蘭辭新考). Being that she was from an elite family, it is possible that she may have known how to write, enabling her to leave the Ballad behind to be discovered later; it is also possible that she uttered these words in the presence of a large crowd. Either way, the Ballad became a sensation, and countless people across China began repeated these touching words.

A memorial to Mulan has been engraved into a large stone located on Mulan Mountain in Hebei, China. The memorial comments that the Ballad was, in fact, composed during Mulan’s lifetime, calling it “Mulan Ci.”

There are two names for the Ballad of Mulan in Chinese: Mulan Shi (木蘭詩) and Mulan Ci (木蘭辭). This difference in naming has been largely ignored by people who assume that Shi and Ci have the same meaning. On the contrary, they are very different. In every Ci which I have ever read, the poet is claiming to write an autobiography. Although there are many Ci where modern scholars doubt that the author is actually writing about himself, it is never contested that the poet at least claims to be the central figure of his own poem. The only exception to this rule is the Ballad of Mulan.

Perhaps the reason why historians have been unable to determine the author and date of the Ballad is that the author has been hiding in plain sight this entire time.



After Mu Lan composed the Ballad, people were so moved by this poem that they began passing it down in oral form for nearly a century before it was finally preserved in written form during the Tang dynasty. Before it was collected into the musical records, it is very possible that several alterations may have been made. In addition to the name Mu Lan changing to Mulan, another significant alteration may have also been introduced.

Mu Lan’s father passed away in 430 AD, the year after she joined the army. Thus, her sacrifice was in vain. However, the conclusion to the Ballad describes Mulan meeting the emperor and declining his offer of a prominent position, only requesting to be reunited with her parents. Because her father had already passed away by the time Mu Lan returned from battle, the joyful conclusion to the Ballad must have been added.

How did Mu Lan’s story actually end?

During the Ming dynasty, historian Zhu Guozhen commented on Mulan’s story, revealing a tragic ending. Zhu Guozhen wrote a collection of reports on various civil and political issues, compiling them into a work which he titled A Minuscule Book from the Yongzhuang Studio. This book has been praised by modern historians (such as 丁富信) for taking great care to preserve historical details.

One particular topic which Zhu Guozhen addressed was that of woman soldiers, heralding Mulan as an exemplary woman who fought valiantly alongside men while still defending her chastity. Zhu Guozhen wrote:

When the royal court finally heard about Mulan’s true nature, the emperor was filled with lust and commanded that she be brought before him so that she could be offered one of the most prominent positions in the royal harem. Mulan spoke eloquently and said, “My body is not suitable for your highness,” vowing that she would rather die. When the emperor tried to take her by force, she ended her own life.
Original Chinese text

Sadly, this tragic conclusion is consistent with Emperor Taiwu’s character. He had no respect for human life, caring only about his conquests. This same ruler, upon discovering that Mu Lan was a woman, would not have shown any respect for her. It would be within his depraved nature to desire to have her for himself.

After Mu Lan returned home from war, she was summoned to appear before the emperor. Rather than being rewarded for her valiant service, a nightmare began unfolding as she learned that she was about to be taken as a concubine.

I believe that at this moment, in the presence of the royal court, Mu Lan began to utter the harrowing words which we now know as the Ballad of Mulan. These lines must have been burning in her heart ever since she heard of her father’s passing. Although it is possible that the conclusion to the Ballad was modified long after her death, she could have introduced the alteration herself. Standing defiantly in front of the emperor, her words began to drip with irony halfway through the poem. She told of how she was rewarded for the years she spent serving in the emperor’s army, which of course did not happen. Everyone present would have felt appalled by the stark contrast with the scene which was transpiring before their very eyes. But as she continued, she admitted that she had no desire for acclaim. The only thing for which she truly longed was to be reunited with her parents. After she had finished uttering the final words of this poem, the emperor was still indifferent, desiring only to have is way with her. When Mu Lan realized that she had no other choice, she took her own life.

What Zhu Guozhen records next is surprising:

Greatly moved, the emperor posthumously promoted her to general and added “filial” to her title.
Original Chinese text

The emperor, whose conscience had already been seared, would not have been disturbed by a woman’s death. He had already grown accustomed to ordering countless soldiers to be slaughtered on a regular basis. He did not have a change of heart; he was trying to save face.

As the Ballad swept across the country, the Chinese people were both alarmed and inspired by Mu Lan’s story. Despite his best efforts, the emperor found himself powerless against the spread of this touching poem which condemned his tyranny. He promoted Mu Lan posthumously and may have even ordered that her story never be recorded in any official form in hopes that he would cease to be perceived as a monster. Still, the Ballad continued to spread.

The real story of Mulan is not a tale of war. It is not about a woman who challenged gender stereotypes and proceeded to single-handedly save China from the barbarians. Rather, the true story of Mulan tells us how a woman pitied her father and was willing to renounce the world to save him from the emperor’s cruel orders. Even though her sacrifice proved to be useless, she refused to be overcome with grief. When the emperor was shamelessly titillated by her story, she resisted his tyranny in bold defiance.

The true story of Mulan is an empowering tale of a woman who simply refused to give up.

When her world came crashing down, she defied the emperor with her dying breath.

She was the hero China needed.
